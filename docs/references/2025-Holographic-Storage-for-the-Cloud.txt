Holographic Storage is an old idea that has always promised high density and fast random access, but has 
never been commercially competitive with Hard Disk Drives (HDDs) and Solid State Devices (SSDs). In Project 
HSD at Microsoft Research we asked the question: “Does holographic storage finally make sense for cloud 
storage?” This article describes our journey toward answering this question. 

We achieved 1.8 × higher density than the previous state-of-the-art, using commodity components available 
today and leveraging machine learning to compensate for the noise and distortions introduced by commodity 
components. This uncovered two new challenges which are the focus of this article: achieving high end-to- 
end energy efficiency without sacrificing capacity, and spatial multiplexing without mechanical movement.

1  Introduction 

Cloud storage is going through an inflection. For warm rewritable storage in the cloud this has 
driven exponential demand for magnetic hard disk drive ( HDD ) capacity. This demand is already 
at exabyte scale and will reach zetabyte scale in the mid-2020s [ 81 ], even sooner than previously 
predicted [ 21 ]. Faced with exponentially increasing demand, cloud providers such as Microsoft 
have relied on corresponding growth in hardware GB/$ to keep costs from exploding. This has 
usually meant increased capacity per device at a more-or-less constant dollar cost per device. How- 
ever, we can no longer rely on this, and if we do not fundamentally innovate in storage hardware 
then the demand will outstrip the supply and the cost of of cloud storage will increase dramatically. 
There has been so much innovation in the last decade in computing and networking, much of 
it driven by the needs and scale of the cloud. Yet, we have seen little fundamental innovation in 
storage. HDDs and SSDs are no longer improving exponentially in GB/$: they are in the mature 
phase of their the technology “S-curve” [ 80 ] shown in Figure 1 . While GB/$ continues to improve 
for these technologies, the rate of improvement is slowing down. It is not clear yet whether new 
technologies such as HAMR will start a new S-curve for HDD density. However, for HDDs in 
particular, an even bigger challenge is that the access rates (IOPS) have been flat for decades. 
Many workloads cannot benefit from improvements in device capacity as they do not come with 
a corresponding increase in device IOPS. 

Our focus in this article is on warm storage, i.e., rewritable storage with a relatively high access 
rate requirement. Other storage tiers such as archival storage are facing similar challenges. The 
incumbent technology — magnetic tape — is also mature and has only been improving slowly. 
DNA storage [ 9 , 18 , 32 ] and more recently storage in glass [ 3 , 4 ] are two examples of disruptive 
new technologies that are being developed to replace magnetic tape. However, these technologies 
will not meet the needs of warm data storage. They are not rewritable and they do not provide 
sufficiently high access rates particularly for small random accesses. On the other hand, they offer 
a level of durability, density, and low cost that is beyond what is needed for warm data.

Warm storage is thus ready for disruption by new storage media. Cloud storage also offers 
a unique opportunity to innovate in overall storage design. HDDs and SSDs initially relied on 
consumer device demand to drive scale and volume: the effect is that even in the cloud, they are 
constrained to use legacy form factors and interfaces, e.g., the 3.5” form factor for HDDs. Cloud 
storage (like computing and networking) by itself is already at a scale that justifies revisiting these 
design decisions. Cloud services are designed at rack scale, and so we can design storage hardware 
for the form factor that makes the most sense for the technology, at least up to rack scale. Both 
hardware and software details are abstracted away from the user, who sees only high-level service 
APIs. This frees us to rethink the software and hardware interfaces and the storage stack. 

Why holographic storage? Holographic storage [ 25 ] records data as holograms in a photosensi- 
tive medium. It is an old idea with an extensive research literature. It has several attractive prop- 
erties for warm data storage: it can use a rewritable, low-cost, bulk 3D medium. Unlike flash, this 
does not wear out — it can sustain an indefinite number of program/erase cycles. It uses optics to 
read and write data, which means it can provide high access rates and low latency. Unlike mag- 
netic media (HDDs), CMOS-based memories (e.g., flash SSDs), or 2-D optical storage (e.g., DVDs), 
rewritable holographic storage can be truly 3-dimensional. A single volume (or “zone”) can in 
theory store thousands of holograms, each of which encodes tens of kilobytes to megabytes of 
data. The optical read/write mechanisms can be decoupled from the bulk media, compared to the 
tight integration required, e.g., with HDD heads which must be in extremely close proximity to 
the medium. Holograms can be randomly accessed for read within each zone. Holograms are read 
and written as entire 2-D images each encoding a large number of symbols, again unlike magnetic 
media where a read/write head can only read a single symbol at a time. These properties make 
holographic storage attractive for durability, density, cost, throughput and IOPS. 

However despite these attractive properties, holographic storage has never been successfully 
commercialized. Even in the research literature, there are few examples of end-to-end systems or 
prototypes for Write Once Read Many ( WORM ) holographic storage; even fewer of rewritable 
holographic storage; and none approaching the capacity, scale, or performance that would be 
needed for commercial viability. In Project HSD [ 64 ] we asked the question “does rewritable holo- 
graphic storage finally make sense in the cloud era”? We note that advances in optical technology 
has enabled high storage densities as well as performance that is orders of magnitude better than 
possible in the past. At the same time, commoditization of these technologies has driven down

Learnings from Project HSD . For the previous wave of research in holographic storage (until the 
early 2000s), achieving high density at low cost was a challenge. The key optical technologies 
— cameras, SLMs (Spatial Light Modulators), and lasers were low-resolution and/or high-cost. In 
Project HSD we demonstrated that high density is achievable using commodity components: we 
have recorded densities [ 13 ] 1.8 × higher than the previous state-of-the-art [ 15 ]. To do this we 
leveraged the commoditization and improvement of optical components in the last two decades. 
We used machine learning to compensate for the distortions added by commodity optics [ 50 ] and 
improved the state-of-the-art in measurement and modeling of holographic media [ 23 ]. 

This uncovered two new challenges that are the focus of this article. First, the end-to-end energy 
efficiency of rewritable holographic storage was 2–3 orders of magnitude too low to be practical. 
While there has been previous research on minimizing the optical energy used for individual reads 
and writes, the challenge is to achieve end-to-end energy efficiency without sacrificing net capacity . 
This means we must consider the overhead caused by media maintenance operations such as read 
refresh and garbage collection that are essential to cope with write erasure and read erasure . In this 
article we describe, model, and optimize end-to-end energy efficiency. Compared to the previous 
state-of-the-art for optimizing media energy use [ 57 ], this improved end-to-end efficiency by 14 ×
for typical workloads without sacrificing capacity. 

Second, we identify optical spatial multiplexing as a key challenge. Multiplexing the camera 
and SLM over a large amount of data is essential for achieving competitive cost per GB. This 
problem is not well addressed in previous research, with most work assuming physical movement 
of the medium. This fundamentally limits IOPS making the technology less attractive than HDDs 
(which already suffer from low IOPS). Thus the challenge is to achieve spatial multiplexing without 
requiring mechanical movement. We describe in this article a mechanical movement free design 
for spatial multiplexing. 

However, neither challenge has been fully solved. Energy efficiency is still a barrier to com- 
mercial viability, and we need another 1–2 orders of magnitude improvement on this metric. We 
believe this is only possible with fundamental advances in holographic materials. Spatial multiplex- 
ing also continues to be a challenge. Our spatial multiplexing design can either achieve good power 
efficiency or high density, but not both simultaneously. Further advances in optics are needed to 
overcome this challenge. 

2  Background 
2.1  Incumbent Technologies: HDDs and SSDs 

Figure 2 shows the historical capacity per device of HDDs. Until around 2010 this was a true 
Moore’s Law exponential driven by shrinking feature sizes. However, since then the rate has 
slowed as improvement is driven by increasingly complex and “one-off” advances such as helium- 
filling to increase platter count; shingling to increase the number of tracks; and most recently 
energy-assisted technologies such as HAMR [ 65 ]. It is not yet clear whether HAMR will allow 
significant further scaling. It is a very complex technology and despite more than a decade of re- 
search, has not yet deployed commercially at scale [ 69 ]. Due to this complexity it is also likely to 
increase per-device cost when it ships. 

The real challenge with magnetic HDDs however is that they are less and less able to sustain the 
access rates (IOPS/TB) required by warm data workloads. A viable technology for cloud storage 
needs to provide an IOPS/TB that is equal or higher than the IOPS/TB of the workload. However, 
HDD IOPS per device have stayed almost completely flat for several decades, which means that

The decline in IOPS/TB results in stranded capacity which is a huge problem for cloud storage 
providers. If the average IOPS/TB of the workload is higher than that of the device, then we cannot 
use the full capacity of the device, and thus the increase in per-device capacity gives no benefit. 
In some cases stranded capacity can be resold as “low-latency archival storage” [ 59 ] — however 
this is a relatively small gain in revenue as it must compete on cost with tape and other archival 
technologies. The only other solution to stranded capacity is to move more of the workload onto 
SSDs at significantly higher cost/GB. 

Will SSDs eventually become cheap enough to solve this problem? Historically SSD costs have 
been driven by the increasing areal density of NAND flash chips. Figure 4 shows this historical 
areal density. For 2D flash, this effectively stopped increasing in 2010 and further increases were 
achieved by increasing the number of 3D flash layers. Scaling in the vertical dimension gives only 
linear benefits (compared to quadratic for 2D features size reduction) and is also limited by the 
aspect ratio achievable with etching and deposition [ 71 , 77 ]. We may have already hit the limits 
of this — recent advances have relied on a multideck approach (also known as “string stacking”) 
that stacks individual devices on top of each other. While this increases the density and amortizes 
some costs at the package and device level, it does not fundamentally reduce the per-bit cost at 
the chip level. 

The only other option to increase bit density in NAND flash is to increase the number of bits per 
cell. TLC (3 bits/cell) is widely deployed in the cloud today — for some workloads QLC (4 bits/cell) 
can be used. However, this only gives a 33% improvement in capacity, is harder to manufacture, 
and has much lower ( ∼ 4 ×) endurance which is challenging for write-heavy workloads. PLC (5 
bits/cell) has been announced but not yet shipped, and the capacity improvement here is even 
smaller (25% over QLC). The fundamental challenge with this approach is that at each step the 
number of programmable levels per cell must be doubled but the gain is only one bit, hence the 
relative capacity gain shrinks with each generation. 

There is thus an increasingly large gap between SSDs and HDDs. It is possible that an entirely

2.2  Holographic Storage 
Holography was discovered in 1948, but needed the development of the laser (discovered in 1960) 
to be used in practical applications. The first holographic storage system was demonstrated in 
1963 [ 49 ]. Since then there has been an extensive literature on different aspects of the optics and 
physics of holographic storage [ 25 ], and a few attempts to build rewritable storage devices start- 
ing from the 70s [ 33 , 39 , 54 ] — the IBM system [ 14 ], designed in the late 90s, to our knowledge 
represents the most complete end-to-end data storage so far, and the highest previously reported 
density for rewritable holographic storage. However, this was never commercialized: in the early 
2000s there were still several commercial and technological obstacles that would have made pro- 
duction and deployment at scale very difficult. HDDs were still in the exponential growth phase 
of their capacity curves making it very difficult for a new technology at the initial stages of the 
S-curve, to compete on cost/capacity. This we believe has now changed; and for cloud storage in 
particular the declining IOPS/TB of HDDs is a huge driver to find alternative technologies with 
higher IOPS/TB but that are still cheaper than flash SSDs. The ability to design at rack scale for 
the cloud, rather than 3.5” form factors also unblocks the use of commodity optical components. 
Perhaps most importantly, the optical technologies that are key to holography have improved 
dramatically in the last two decades. Cameras in particular have become commodity devices with 
Moore’s Law like improvements driven by the smartphone industry (Figure 5 ). Similarly commodi- 
tization of SLMs which are essentially miniaturized projection devices has been driven by advances 
in heads-up displays, projectors, and LIDAR. Laser quality and power as well as miniaturization 
have been driven by extensive use in optical communications as well as consumer devices. The 
components that were expensive, custom devices in 2000 are now available as commodity devices 
with higher resolution, speed, and quality at lower power and cost. 

3  Holographic Storage Design 

The core principle of holographic data storage is the recording of an optical interference pattern 
(a hologram ) within a photorefractive material. Figure 6 shows hologram formation and readout in 
2 dimensions. 1 An interference pattern is created by the intersection of two coherent laser beams

within the medium (Figure 6 (b)). This pattern results in local changes in refractive index, which 
persist after the light is removed (Figure 6 (c)). To exploit this for storage, one of the beams is 
modulated with a 2D image that encodes a block (page) of data. This data beam is intersected with 
a reference beam to create the hologram. Once the hologram is written, the reference beam alone 
is used for readout — it diffracts off the hologram to recreate a noisy and attenuated version of the 
data beam from which the data can be decoded (Figure 6 (d)). This kind of diffraction is known as 
Bragg diffraction [ 12 ] and the holograms are sometimes referred to as Bragg gratings. 

Only certain materials have the right combination of properties to record holograms. The 
medium must be photosensitive , i.e., respond to light by changing its local optical properties (e.g., 
the refractive index) as a function of light intensity. One popular mechanism is for the light energy 
to cause a chemical change (such as polymerization) changing its refractive index. It fits well for 
WORM holographic storage but it is too slow, energy-intensive, and difficult to reverse to be useful 
for rewritable storage with high access rates. 

For rewritable storage, the best known materials are electro-optical ones. Incident light on such 
materials results in a locally non-uniform electric field, which in turn causes a non-uniform refrac- 
tive index, i.e., a Bragg grating. The most promising — and most studied — electro-optical holo- 
graphic material is iron-doped Lithium Niobate (Fe:LiNbO3) [ 25 ], which has good optical quality 
of holograms, speed of hologram formation, and density of holograms. It is manufactured in bulk 
using the Czochralski method [ 79 ] that is also used to manufacture semiconductor grade silicon, 
and thus has the potential for large-scale production. It is also very stable, and can be read and 
written to for decades without degradation; this is unlike, for example, flash memory which has a 
limited number of write cycles. It also has high durability, i.e., the rate of dark decay (the degrada- 
tion of holograms over time without external action) is very low: a hologram’s half-life is counted 
in years [ 38 ]. In this article we focus on iron-doped Lithium Niobate crystal, which we will refer 
to as the medium or simply the crystal. 

This material is a crystal lattice of Lithium, Niobium, and Oxygen atoms with iron impurities 
( Fe 2 +and Fe 3 +ions) as a dopant material. Lithium Niobate has the required electro-optical prop- 
erties and the Fe ions act as electron traps (Figure 7 . Light at 532 nm wavelength (green) excites 
electrons from Fe 2 +ions to the conduction band where they can diffuse freely. As this excitation 
happens more when the intensity of light is high, the net effect under non-uniform illumination is 
that electrons diffuse from the brightly lit areas to the darker areas. Some of the electrons moved 
to the dark areas of the interference pattern fall into these traps, converting Fe 3 +to Fe 2 +. This

Zoned storage . Holograms once written cannot be overwritten, but new holograms can be super- 
posed on existing ones. All the superposed holograms can be simultaneously erased using lower- 
wavelength (UV) light, generated from low-cost UV LEDs. In a practical system, a larger volume 
of crystal would be subdivided into many zones , each of which can be independently erased. This 
is very similar to the zoned storage model [ 85 ] of Flash SSDs and shingled (SMR) hard disk drives. 
Pages can be appended to a zone, individual pages within a zone can be randomly access for read, 
and the entire zone can be erased (reset). To present the abstraction of rewritable storage, zoned 
storage devices must implement garbage collection to move live data from a zone that is about to 
be erased into a fresh zone. 

Superposition of holograms . Individual holograms can encode kilobytes to megabytes of data 
within a relatively small volume of crystal, i.e., a zone. To achieve high density we need to super- 
pose 100s to 1000s of hologram within each zone. This is done by changing the reference beam 
— for each unique reference beam, a different page can be written to the same zone, and these 
pages can be individually addressed by using the same unique reference beam. For this reason the

Multiple techniques exist for multiplexing within a zone. Angular multiplexing (Figure 8 changes 
the wavefront angle of the reference beam using, e.g., MEMS mirrors and lenses. This is known to 
give very high orthogonality through Bragg selectivity [ 12 ] and has been used successfully to write 
up to 5,000 holograms in a single zone [ 55 ]. Phase multiplexing uses spatial light modulators 
( SLMs ) to encode one of a set of orthogonal phase codes on the reference beam [ 8 , 27 , 29 , 30 , 58 ]. 
Wavelength multiplexing uses different wavelengths of light for different pages [ 49 , 62 , 83 ]. In this 
article we focus on angular multiplexing, which is the most studied and most promising technique 
for high-density holographic storage. 

In principle, the number of holograms that can be superposed with angular multiplexing is only 
limited by the available optical angle and the wavelength of the light. With a typical setup, this is 
around 3,000 holograms. In reality however, the hologram density is limited by the medium, which 
has limited dynamic range and also suffers from both write and read erasure. A common approx- 
imation is that the diffraction efficiency is inversely proportional to the square of the number of 
holograms superposed. However, this is a simplification — the actual relationship is more complex 
and depends on the write and read profiles used for each hologram. These profiles — as well as 
media maintenance operations such as garbage collection and read refresh — govern a complex 
tradeoff between density and energy efficiency. 

Write erasure . Each superposed hologram slightly degrades the previously written holograms 
and decreases their diffraction efficiency. The optimal energy to use for each write depends on the 
number of writes before and after it in that zone; and also on the number of reads we want to 
support on the zone before doing a refresh operation. 

Read erasure . Reads use the same laser light as writes but typically at much lower power levels. 
Thus they also slightly degrade all the superposed holograms in the zone, including theo one 
currently being read. While the erasure from a single read is much lower than that of a write, the 
cumulative erasure of a large number of reads can be significant. As holograms get weaker due to 
read erasure, they require increasing amounts of energy to read. For a fixed laser power, this means 
an increased read latency and reduced IOPS. In practice, this means that we must periodically do a 
read refresh — copy all live data out of a zone that has served many reads into a fresh zone. While 
previous work on holography assumed a constant amount of energy per read, we have found that 
it is much more efficient to use a variable read profile with the energy per read increasing over 
time until a refresh is triggered.

Zone life cycle . We can think of each zone as having a life cycle from when the zone has just been 
erased until it again reaches a fully erased state. The lifecycle consists of a write phase in which 
holograms are written in an append-only manner, followed by a read phase where any page can be 
read at random at any time. For simplicity, we assume that reads will not interleave with writes. 
This is a valid assumption for a design where a small number of open zones is maintained for 
writing per device, and partially written zones are buffered in DRAM or SSD. Interleaving reads 
and writes would add some complexity but we do not believe it would significantly change the 
end-to-end energy efficiency of the system. Figure 9 shows the “life cycle” of a single hologram 
within a zone in a holographic storage system. The top of the figure shows the diffraction efficiency 
of a single page in the zone, starting from when it is written and progressing through writes of 
subsequent pages and the read phase. The diffraction efficiency of the hologram increases over 
time during the write as a function of the total write energy, but with diminishing returns. Reads 
to any hologram in the zone also cause erasure, but at a lower rate as reads typically use less 
energy. Eventually, the hologram must be refreshed by rewriting the data to a different zone. For 
write-heavy workloads, it is also possible that the entire zone is garbage-collected (giving us a 
free refresh of all live data in the zone) before the refresh threshold is reached. The bottom part 
of the figure shows the write profile with the page under consideration in blue and other pages in 
grey. Note that the shape of the read profile is independent of the actual pages being read as read 
erasure applies uniformly across the whole zone. 

3.2  Holographic Translation Layer 

Any practical holographic storage must be able to optimize the write and read profiles as well as 
handle the media management operations (garbage collection and read refresh) in a way that is 
transparent to the application. Figure 10 shows the design that we will use as our system model 
throughout this article. It includes a Holographic Translation Layer ( HTL ) (Figure 10 (a)) that 
provides the abstraction of a rewritable, flat address space on a medium that needs variable write 
and read profiles as well as garbage collection and read refresh. This is similar to the notion of a 
Flash Translation Layer ( FTL ) in flash SSDs [ 1 , 19 , 20 , 31 , 44 , 47 , 66 ]. 

The HTL manages the life cycle of each page in each zone in a device. It tracks the history of 
each zone and makes the key decisions on how much energy to use for each write and read; when 
to refresh a page; and when to trigger garbage collection. We use a set of simple, parametrized 
heuristics to make these decisions. Our approach is then to use a combination of measurement, 
simulation, and modeling to find optimal values of these parameters for any given workload.

Read profiles and write profiles can be computed on a per-zone basis, but for simplicity here we 
assume that they are computed offline as a single read and write profile for the entire system. The 
online read/write energy computation is then a simple index lookup onto the profile. Only a small 
number of zones is kept active for write (i.e., append) at any given time. This allows us to buffer 
all writes until we have an entire zone’s worth of data, and only then to write the zones. This also 
means that we do not suffer read erasure during the write phase. 

Refresh decisions are based on tracking the diffraction efficiency of reads, and rewriting the 
data when this goes below a threshold. In our simplified system model we precompute a fixed read 
budget per zone. We then maintain the invariant that the read budget of a zone always exceeds 
the number of live pages in it; i.e., each live pages can be read at least once more. If this threshold 
is reached then each subsequent read from the zone will trigger a refresh, causing the number of 
live pages in the read zone to decrease. When the number of live pages reaches zero, the zone is 
scheduled for UV-erasure. 

Application writes and refresh writes both create garbage, i.e., the old version of the data that is 
being overwritten. GC frequency is governed by the amount of free space reserved for garbage col- 
lection; when the free space reaches this threshold, the zone with the fewest live pages is selected 
and the live pages are copied to another zone. 

We keep only a few zones open for writing at any time, and the rest are in a read-only (or 
empty) state. We assume that the writeable zones are cached in a higher storage tier (e.g., DRAM 
or flash SSD) and flushed in their entirety when they are full: this means zones transition from 
append-only to read-only without interleaving reads and writes. 

Many variations on these heuristics are possible, and there are also interesting low-level design 
choices, e.g., such as the division of HTL functionality between firmware and system software. 
We believe it is premature to optimize these details before the overall system is shown to be fea- 
sible for energy efficiency. We use these simple heuristics and an idealized system (e.g., we ignore 
concurrency and assume all operations are done sequentially) to explore the parameter space for 
read/write energy, read refresh, and garbage collection in simulation. Regardless of implementa- 
tion details, the key challenge for the HTL is to balance the competing demands of application 
reads and writes with the background media maintenance operations.

system. First, we need to achieve sufficiently high density and energy efficiency to be competitive

4  Energy Efficiency 
By writing and reading entire images at a time, holographic storage exploits the inherent par- 
allelism of SLMs and cameras to achieve high data rates. At the same time, techniques such as 
angular multiplexing allow for rapid addressing by only steering optical beams. In principle this 
should lead to very high IOPS, limited only by the frame rates of the optical devices, e.g., cameras. 
We find however that holographic storage IOPS are limited by the end-to-end energy efficiency : 
the amount of energy required per IO, including the amortized cost of read refresh and garbage

A straightforward way to increase the IOPS would be to drastically reduce the density of holo-

energy efficiency of the system without reducing density. 

We believe laser efficiency can improve from 2% to around 15% and that the cost per Watt of

room for improvement. Read energy could be decreased by increasing camera sensitivity but this is 
likely to trade off signal-to-noise ratio (and hence effective capacity) for performance. Overall, for 
holographic storage to be competitive, we still need a significant reduction in the HTL overhead 
(garbage collection and read refresh) as well as the energy efficiency at the crystal (the average 
energy used per read and per write). 

4.1  Density 

The challenge with holographic storage is to achieve high end-to-end energy efficiency as well 
as high density. The best previous density achieved (by IBM) was 5.8 GB/cm 3 [ 15 ], with 1,000 
pages using precisely aligned custom optical components. This density is sufficient to consider 
holographic storage seriously as a warm storage technology; however it was not clear if it was 
realistic for an end-to-end scenario and whether we could leverage commodity cameras and SLMs.

the number of user pages net of error correction and also net of any free space set aside for garbage

capacity by overprovisioning the raw capacity. i.e., while a higher raw density does have a penalty 
in diffraction efficiency, it is more than compensated for by less frequent garbage collection.

4.3  Write and Read Profile Optimization 

The “life cycle” of a zone (Figure 9 ), can be divided into a write (append) phase during which new 
holograms are added to the zone, and a read phase during which the holograms are read. For each 
zone lifecycle, we can choose the length of the write phase (i.e., the number of raw pages per 
zone) and the length of the read phase (the number of reads that must be supported on a zone 
before the data is rewritten). In addition, we can tune the amount of energy used for each write 
and each read in these two phases. The optimal values of these profile parameters are a function 
of the crystal properties (sensitivity and erasure) as well as the workload properties. We map this 
to an optimization problem where the inputs are the desired user capacity in pages per zone, and 
the workload. The outputs are the optimal write profile and read profile. The length of the write 
profile is the number of raw pages per zone; typically this is larger than the target user capacity 
per zone to allow for garbage collection. The length of the read profile determines the point at 
which the zone must be refreshed to avoid read erasure. 

There is a complex tradeoff between the HTL parameters and the read and write profiles, and 
energy efficiency. The end-to-end efficiency depends on the amount of free space reserved for 
garbage collection, the frequency of read refresh, the frequency of garbage collection, the energy 
per read, and the energy per write. Our first challenge therefore was to model the energy con- 
sumption of the entire zone lifecycle as a function of the write and read profiles and the workload. 
To do this we derived an analytic model of garbage collection and read refresh frequency, and 
combined it with the read/write sensitivity and erasure equations. Our analytic model assumes a 
uniform random read/write workload that is parametrized by the read/write ratio. 

A second challenge is that the search space of write and read profiles is extremely large, making 
it infeasible for most optimization search techniques. From our analytic model we were able to 
express the energy consumption of the entire zone lifecycle as a function of just three parameters: 
the energy of the first write; the number of writes (i.e., the number of raw pages per zone); and the 
number of reads of the zone before a read refresh (the “read budget”). Table 1 shows the qualitative 
effect of each of these parameters on the HTL overhead and read/write energy at the crystal. This 
made it possible to use a standard genetic algorithm, NSGA-II [ 72 ] to search for optimal points. 
However, it was still slow due to the high cost of evaluating a single point, which took about 
0.3s. By reformulating the problem to eliminate expensive root-finding inner loops and porting 
the implementation from Python to C++, we improved this by four orders of magnitude, to 0.3ms 
per evaluation. This made it feasible to find optimized solutions in about 15s–1min on a 20-core 
desktop machine. This means we can sweep the space of workload parameters, different media, and 
other system parameters rapidly while using the per-zone energy optimization as an inner loop.

4.4  End-to-end Optimization 
End-to-end optimization of read and write profiles (i.e., including HTL overheads in the optimiza- 
tion loop) significantly improves the energy efficiency of the system. We compare this approach 
to the previous state of the art state-of-the-art [ 57 ] where the write profile was chosen to achieve 
equal diffraction efficiency for all holograms at the end of the write phase, chosen to be 60% of 
the maximum achievable diffraction efficiency for the same number of superposed holograms on 
a given crystal. Intuitively this approach is aiming for the “knee” of the diminishing returns curve 
in Figure 15 (a). 

Our approach improves on the previous one in three ways. First, we use minimal reads that use 
only the minimum energy required at the current diffraction efficiency of holograms in the zone. 
This significantly extends the lifetime of the zone before read refresh is required. Second we jointly 
optimize the write and read profiles using our improved media erasure model. Finally, we use our 
analytic model of GC/refresh to do end-to-end optimization that includes the overhead of these 
media management operations. This lets us optimize an additional key parameter — the number 
of raw pages per zone. A larger number of raw pages reduces the frequency of GC (as we have 
more free space to absorb writes), but increases the impact of erasure as well as the overhead of 
copying zones for GC and read refresh. 

We evaluate the contribution of each of these improvements by comparing four configurations. 
We choose as a baseline a target user page capacity of 360 pages/zone, which is 50% of our maxi- 
mum raw density — this allows us to explore overprovisioning as a way to reduce GC overhead. 
We use a uniform random workload with a read/write ratio varying from 0% to 100%. We compare 
the following configurations: 

—Baseline: write profile with a fixed (60%) diffraction efficiency target and flat read profile, 

and a fixed allocation of 10% free space for GC (i.e., 400 raw pages per zone). 

—Minimal reads: same as the baseline, but using minimal reads. 
—Jointly optimized: write and read profiles jointly optimized using our new method, but raw 

pages per zone still fixed. 

—End-to-end optimized: write and read profiles and the number of raw pages per zone were 

all jointly optimized.

4.5  Read Validation 
While diffraction efficiency is the key metric for measuring hologram strength, for a storage system 
we need to ensure not just that holograms are sufficiently strong, but also that the data can be read 
back reliably at a given level of error correction. We have validated this experimentally using the 
write and read profiles generated from end-to-end optimization, for both 50% reads and 100% reads. 
In both cases, we were able to read back pages up to the end of the generated read profile (i.e., up 
to the point of read refresh) with a BER (bit error rate) that was correctable with the configured 
amount of error correction. Further details on the validation experiments are in our companion 
publication [ 23 ]. While a fully deployed system would require much more validation — this gives 
us confidence that our profile optimization method gives feasible configurations.

4.8  Improving Efficiency Further 
Is a 14 × improvement over the state-of-the-art sufficient to make holographic storage competitive? 
Unfortunately it does not, even with optimistic assumptions on technology improvements. We 
assume that laser efficiency would improve from 2% to 15% and also that laser costs will drop 
significantly with volume production. Based on input from laser vendors, our estimate for laser 
cost at volume is $400 per optical Watt of output power (or $60 per electrical Watt at 15% efficiency). 
We also assume that the other components (cameras, SLMs, crystal) contribute negligibly to optical 
power loss, electrical power consumption, and dollar cost. This is very optimistic, especially in the 
case of spatial multiplexing, described in the next section. Even under these assumptions, our 
analysis shows that we need at least another  13 × improvement in end-to-end energy efficiency to 
be cost-competitive with HDDs. In reality, given the non-zero contribution of other components to 
cost and power and the challenges of spatial multiplexing, we need an even larger improvement. 
In our view, this can only be achieved by fundamental improvements in the physical medium. 
In other words, despite the attractive properties of iron-doped Lithium Niobate, it is unlikely to be 
the way forwards for holographic storage. At minimum we need new materials; possibly we need

5  Spatial Multiplexing 

A naive implementation of holographic storage would allocate one camera and one SLM to each 
zone of the medium. However, this is not practical from a cost point of view. While the bulk medium 
is cheap, the cost of the camera and SLM is non-negligible and needs to be amortized over tens of 
terabytes of capacity. On the other hand, several factors limit the capacity of a single zone to tens 
of megabytes. Write erasure limits the number of pages that can be written to a single zone, and 
the SLM and camera resolution limits the size of each page. These limitations cannot be addressed 
simply by using larger zones. Instead, we must scale by multiplexing a single camera-SLM pair 
over a large number of relatively small zones spread over the bulk medium. Each zone would be a 
few millimeters in dimension, and we would need thousands to millions of such zones per camera- 
SLM pair. We refer to this multiplexing as spatial multiplexing [ 16 ] as distinct from multiplexing 
within a zone, e.g., angular multiplexing. 

Previous approaches assumed the crystal would move with respect to the optics to allow access 
to different zones [ 5 , 60 ]. e.g., by using a circular “platter” like design which would both rotate and 
translate. This approach is not practical for high IOPS workloads, as the mechanical movement 
would limit the number of IOPS that could be achieved. HDDs — which are heavily optimized 
for rotational and seek latency — are already struggling to keep up with the IOPS demands of 
cloud storage. Holographic crystal is much heavier than HDD media making its movement at 
speed (while limiting vibrations and forces exerted on the medium) much more challenging than 
HDDs’ platters. The alternative — moving the optical components such as cameras and SLMs with 
respect to a fixed medium – is also impractical. The cost advantages of using commodity optical 
components come with the disadvantage of relatively large size and weight. The only way to 
achieve high IOPS is to move light rather than the mass, i.e., steer the optical beams to the correct 
zones. 

We considered a design based on MEMS mirrors and a single scan lens [ 34 ]. However, using 
available lenses this design is fundamentally limited to a few tens of zones [ 16 ]. We decided instead 
to rethink spatial multiplexing from scratch, and converged on an “optical imaging network” that 
uses waveguides to transport entire images along a straight line path. It uses binary switching 
elements composed of pi-cells [ 10 ] and polarized beam splitters [ 40 , 82 ]. Each switching element 
can either transmit the image along the waveguide, or reflect it at right angles to the original 
direction. Using these elements, we can imagine networks that transport SLM images from a single 
SLM to one of many zones; and conversely from one of many zones to a single camera. 

Waveguide propagation is almost lossless but the switching elements introduce losses. Even 
a small loss per element can add up to a large loss if the image must propagate through many 
elements; thus the number of switching elements on the path is a key metric. We investigated 
multiple network designs with different tradeoffs on cost/complexity, physical volume, and optical 
power loss. 

A binary tree configuration (Figure 21 (a)) is optimal in terms of losses. However, it is complex 
to manufacture and crucially it has an extremely large physical footprint. To multiplex over 1 M 
zones (about 28 TB with our current optical components) requires three trees each of depth 20: one 
each for the data beam, the address beam, and the readout beam. As imaging waveguides (unlike 
fibers) can only propagate light in a straight line, and the cross-section of the waveguide is equal 
to the width of the zone, this means that the volume of the waveguide network is 120 times that of 
the crystal it addresses. This is a loss of 120 × in effective density, making the design unattractive 
for rack density which is a key metric for cloud storage.

6  Related Work 

Holography was first described in 1948 by Gabor [ 35 , 36 , 37 ]. In the 1960s, the concept of Bragg 
selectivity emerged [ 45 , 49 , 75 ], enabling precise readout of a single hologram from a volume 
containing multiple superimposed holograms. This ability to store multiple holograms in the same 
volume made holography attractive for data storage, not just for images. 

The theory, physics, and optics of holographic storage have an extensive literature. There have 
been a few end-to-end storage prototypes and no commercially successful products. Our aim in 
this section is not to provide a comprehensive review of the literature, but to highlight the key 
works that have influenced our research and to provide context for our contributions. For more 
detail, we recommend the book Holographic Data Storage [ 25 ] as an excellent introduction to the 
history and fundamentals of the field as well as a comprehensive review of the literature up to the 
year 2000. 

6.1  Rewritable Holographic Storage 

Early attempts at high-density rewritable holographic storage were severely limited by the avail- 
able hardware components. One of the earliest uses of angle multiplexing from the 70s [ 33 ] was 
limited to 10 pages of 10 Kb each. Other prototypes from the 70s [ 54 ] showed the ability to erase 
and rewrite the crystal without degrading it. 

The most complete and highest density rewritable holographic data storage prototypes we are 
aware of are the IBM DEMON AND DEMON 2 [ 14 , 15 , 68 ], designed in the late 90s. This system 
used an SLM with 1024 ×1024 pixels and matching cameras with very precise alignment to store 

ACM Trans. Storage, Vol. 21, No. 1, Article 3. Publication date: January 2025. 

3:24 

N. Cheriere et al. 

large holograms. They relied on an error correction code that encoded 8 bits in 12 pixels, resulting 
in a page size of 670,816 user bits. By angle-multiplexing 1,000 holograms in a single zone of 
(1.6 ×1 . 6 ×5.5 mm), they demonstrated a net density after error correction of 44 Gb/cm 3 . At these 
densities it finally became feasible to think about holographic storage as a competitor to HDDs. 
However, it was still limited by the high cost of the custom optics; the need for precise alignment; 
the need to compete with HDDs in their Moore’s Law phase; and the very low energy efficiency. 
The system was never commercialized. 

6.2  WORM Holographic Storage 

Holographic storage has been more widely studied and prototyped for write-once, read-many 
( WORM ) applications such as archival storage. These typically use photosensitive polymer media, 
often in a relatively thin film coated on a spinning substrate, i.e., an optical disk form factor. The 
media manufacture is even cheaper than for rewritable media, and the energy costs for read are 
lower. The use of mechanical movement (i.e., rotation and seek) limits IOPS, but this is less of an 
issue for archival storage than for warm storage. IOPS requirements are typically lower for archival 
storage (the competition is magnetic tape), and latency is often dominated by other factors (such 
as retrieval from a library). 

Several such systems  [ 51 , 53 , 63 , 74 ] have been proposed to compete with archival media such 
as optical DVDs but few have been commercialized. One of the earliest attempts in this space is 
from the 60s [ 51 ] which wrote 4 sets of 4,096 holograms, each with 32 ×32 bits that could be read by 
a single device. This would result in a storage capacity of 2 MB per disk with a reported bandwidth 
of 16 MB/s. The density was relatively low as angle multiplexing had not been developed yet. 

Holoscan [ 74 ] (1974) is, as far as we know, the only holographic data storage system to reach 
commercialization. It was a read only sequential memory intended for checks verification. It 
had a capacity of 1.5 MB and a read bandwidth of 112 Kb/s. In the late 90s Tamarack Multi- 
store [ 63 ] and others [ 53 ] proposed systems with capacities in the 500 MB — 1 GB range per 
disk. More recently InPhase [ 7 , 26 ] demonstrated the storage of 300 GB in a single disk (CD- 
ROM shaped, 1.5 mm thick medium) and reached a throughput of 20 MB/s. However, this did 
not succeed commercially and currently, there is no commercially available holographic storage 
technology. 

Write-only holographic storage for archival applications continues to be an active area of re- 
search. Current proposals aim for an optical disk form factor with a capacity of 1 TB and a through- 
put of 1-10 Gb/s [ 42 ]. However the capacity is still not high enough to be competitive, and the 
lifetime of the polymer materials used for WORM holographic storage is still a concern, compared 
to other more durable materials, e.g., glass [ 3 , 4 ]. 

6.3  Photorefractive Materials 

The  atomic  changes  happening  during  hologram  formation  have  been  modeled  by 
Kukhtarev  et  al.  [ 46 ].  This  model  describes  the  movement  of  electrons  in  the  material  and 
is the foundation of metrics used to evaluate materials, including the sensitivity of a material or 
the erasure constant that describe how easy is it to record a hologram and how stable it is when 
read. Mok et al. [ 57 ] introduce M# (read “M number”), a characteristic of the material that links 
the number of holograms superimposed M to their diffraction efficiency η = (M# )2 /m 2 . 

Photorefractive materials have been studied in details and the results have been compiled in 
books such as Lithium Niobate: Defects, Photorefraction and Ferroelectric Switching [ 76 ] for lithium 
niobate in particular or Photorefractive Materials and Their Applications 2: Materials [ 38 ] for a range 
of materials covering polymers as well as inorganic crystals.

The fact that Lithium Niobate has a tradeoff between write sensitivity and erasure is well 
known [ 41 ]. While end-to-end optimization can improve the tradeoff significantly as shown by 
our work, this tradeoff still exists and continues to be a challenge in making holographic storage 
competitive with other storage technologies. 

6.4  Beam Configuration 
There have been several attempts to optimize holographic storage by optimizing the optical beam 
properties. From a theoretical perspective, density is maximized when the two beams intersect 
at right angles to each other in the medium [ 11 ]. Alternatively using a “transmission geometry”
where the two beams intersect at a very narrow angle, can significantly improve write sensitivity 
at the cost of much lower density. In our work, we primarily use the 90-degree configuration as 
our goal was to optimize energy without losing density . It would be interesting to extend our media 
model and end-to-end optimization to include transmission geometry as a design parameter. 

Write efficiency for a small number of holograms can also be improved by using a higher in- 
tensity of laser beam [ 70 ] to exploit non-linearities in the hologram formation process. However, 
this effect is not significant when writing many hundreds of holograms in a zone. In practice, 
we already use the maximum intensity possible from the available laser and optics — even if this 
does not improve energy efficiency significantly, it reduces the time to deliver the same amount 
of energy and hence improves IOPS. 

6.5  Phase Multiplexing 
Angle multiplexing [ 17 , 48 , 49 , 56 , 73 ] is the most common method of superposing multiple holo- 
grams in the same zone, by changing the angle of the reference beam and exploiting Bragg selec- 
tivity [ 45 , 49 , 75 ] to minimize cross-talk between the different holograms. 

Phase codes [ 8 , 27 , 29 , 30 , 58 ] are another method to superimpose multiple holograms in the 
same zone. The reference beam is modulated using an SLM. This reduces system complexity by 
avoiding a dependency on fast and precise MEMS mirrors — we can simply use SLMs, which are al- 
ready being used for the data beam. In theory, orthogonal phase codes such as Walsh–Hadamard 
codes can have lower cross-talk and a better signal-to-noise ratio than angle multiplexing [ 27 ]. 
After extensive experimentation we found that in practice, commodity SLMs can not exactly im- 
plement ideal phase codes and this results in higher cross-talk and noise than angle multiplexing. 
We were able to write 500 pages at 47 KB each, slightly better than the previous state-of-the-art 
of 480 × 37.5 KB for phase multiplexing [ 29 ]. However, this is lower than we can write with angle 
multiplexing; we also saw higher erasure and much higher bit error rate ( BER ) on readout than 
with angle multiplexing. It is possible that with better SLMs, phase multiplexing could be com- 
petitive or even surpass angle multiplexing on density; we do not expect the energy efficiency to 
improve however. 

6.6  Spatial Multiplexing 
To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to explore mechanical movement free spatial multi- 
plexing for holographic data storage, and the first to develop an optical waveguide based switch- 
ing network for image transport. Certainly all previous attempts at spatial multiplexing for holo- 
graphic storage relied on mechanical movement. DEMON and DEMON 2 [ 14 , 15 , 68 ] relied on 
movement of the medium to address multiple zones. Tamarack Multistore [ 63 ] also moved the op- 
tics radially (except the laser) in a manner similar to disk readers. Burr et al. [ 16 ] used a mechanical 
scanner to address 16 different volumes each storing 10,000 holograms.

6.7  Translation Layers and Garbage Collection 
The FTL is a key component in flash-based storage systems. It is responsible for mapping logical 
addresses to physical addresses, garbage collection, and wear leveling. FTLs balance multiple ob- 
jectives [ 1 ], including maximizing the lifetime [ 19 , 20 ] of the flash memory, maximizing storage 
space [ 44 ], and improving performance [ 31 , 47 , 66 ]. Our HTL design is analogous to an FTL and 
occupies a similar place in the storage stack. However, the optimization parameters are different, 
in particular the value of the HTL is in jointly optimizing the media energy profiles with garbage 
collection. Unlike flash, read refresh is a crucial management operation, whereas wear leveling 
is not. 

7  Discussion 

The focus of this article has been on energy-efficiency and spatial multiplexing as these were 
(and still are) the main challenges to solve to make the technology practical as cloud storage. 
In the course of doing this research, we have also identified some interesting areas for future 
investigation, both in the context of holographic storage and in the broader context of storage 
systems. 

Machine learning . We have used machine learning in Project HSD to compensate for the dis- 
tortions and aberrations introduced by commodity optical components. In particular, ML helps us 
solve problems related to alignment , i.e., the mapping between image pixels and user data bits. 
Without alignment, the data is not decodable at all, even with large amounts of error correction. 
ML decode also lets us encode 2 bits per image symbol where conventional signal processing only 
lets us decode 1 bit per symbol. 

The use of convolutional neural nets ( CNNSs ) for processing images is well established, 
and as the fundamental unit of holographic read and write is the image, we are able to leverage 
the existing work on CNNs. We are also able to exploit recent and ongoing advances in machine 
learning hardware, especially inference-optimized hardware. While the use of ML on the data path 
would have been unthinkable a decade ago, today we have hardware and models that can decode 
data at high data rates and modest hardware cost and power. These models can correct for complex 
effects such as waveguide propagation that are extremely difficult to compensate with traditional 
physics-based approaches. 

While other storage hardware will potentially have different sources of error and distortion, we 
believe this is a promising area of investigation for optical image based data storage in particular 
and for all kinds of storage in general. 

Beyond image processing, we have also found machine learning, in particular Bayesian opti- 
mization [ 78 ] useful as a design tool. We have used Bayesian optimization to optimize hardware 
settings on the write path, specifically the intensity values to use on the SLM for each symbol value 
to be encoded. We have also used Bayesian optimization as an alternative to genetic algorithms for 
our end-to-end optimization of HTL and media parameters. Bayesian optimization is much more 
sample-efficient than the genetic approach, i.e., it evaluates far fewer points while searching for the 
optimal solution. However, it is also more computationally expensive per point generated. Overall 
the genetic algorithm was clearly the right choice when using our fast analytic model of the system 
— it converged an order of magnitude faster in real time than the Bayesian approach. However, 
we believe there is value in investigating the Bayesian approach in more depth, in particular when 
using expensive simulation models where the computational cost per sample is dominated by the 
simulation itself. 

Full-stack optimization . So far we have focused on the challenges and opportunities for opti- 
mization below a block (or page) read/write storage interface. e.g., on jointly optimizing the HTL

parameters and the media parameters. However, we believe there is also significant potential for 
joint optimization across more layers of the storage stack. e.g., log-structured layouts such as LSM 
trees [ 22 , 43 , 61 ] are a natural fit for zoned storage. Many variants of LSM trees exist, offering a 
tradeoff between capacity, write amplification, and read amplification [ 6 , 28 , 52 ]. As similar trade- 
offs exist at the HTL and at the crystal level, it would be interesting to consider if making the 
optimization even more “end-to-end” by including the application data structure, could improve 
the overall system performance. 

Adaptive hardware . In Project HSD we started with a clean-slate design — to maximize the ben- 
efit of designing for the cloud free of legacy constraints. This has allowed us to optimize across 
layers in a way that would not be possible otherwise. e.g., there is no way to exploit workload 
knowledge to change the low-level parameters of a 3.5” HDD such as the amount of energy used 
per read or per write. With the HSD design we can adapt the low-level parameters such as the 
read/write energy profiles to the workload, and this has been crucial to the energy efficiency im- 
provements. We could also adapt different zones within the same device differently, e.g., to support 
different workloads or different SLAs — we have not explored this direction but it is an interesting 
area for future research. 

Tail latency . Latency distributions and tail latency are important metrics for warm storage. We 
have not considered them in our analysis so far — in our model we have a single powerful laser 
that is multiplexed over a large amount of data. The multiplexing can be done rapidly; and at full 
power the latency of individual reads and writes is small, even for high-energy operations (i.e., at 
the beginning of the write profile and the end of the read profile). It is likely that further down the 
line — if/when the challenges of energy efficiency and scalable spatial multiplexing are solved —
we will need to revisit the issue of latency. In particular, if a large number of small lasers is more 
cost-effective than a single large multiplexed laser, then latency may prove to be a new challenge. 

Optimizing for skewed workloads . The work we presented is focused around uniform random 
workloads. However, in practice, workloads are often skewed, which open up new opportunities 
for optimization as the device can be reconfigured for different workloads at runtime. For example, 
pages written could be classified according to their read and write patterns, and then written to 
different zones with different media parameters in order to maximize the efficiency of the device. 
While this is an interesting research area, we expect that at best, this would still be limited by the 
best achievable energy efficiency for a known read/write ratio (Figure 19 ), which by itself is too 
low to be competitive. 

8  Conclusion 
Rewritable holographic storage is in principle a great solution to the challenges faced by warm 
cloud storage. Previous attempts to commercialize this technology (until about 20 years ago) were 
limited by several factors. HDD capacity per dollar was still growing exponentially; cameras and 
SLMs were slow, expensive, and low-resolution; it is not possible to design practical holographic 
systems in consumer device form factors. 

In Project HSD we have re-examined holographic storage as a cloud-first technology, leverag- 
ing commodity optical components, machine learning, and the freedom to redesign the storage 
stack from top to bottom. We have found that in today’s world the main challenges to solve for 
holographic storage are energy efficiency and spatial multiplexing. While we have made advances 
in both these areas, including a 14 × improvement in end-to-end energy efficiency, these remain 
the primary challenges. 

To make holographic storage practical, we need significant improvements in the medium, i.e., 
new materials that preserve all the advantages of iron-doped Lithium Niobate but with 1–2 orders

of magnitude higher energy efficiency. We also need a spatial multiplexing system that is scalable, 
low-loss, and compact. While there are other interesting research problems to be solved in the 
context of holographic storage, we believe that these two are the most critical to solve before 
revisiting the viability of holography as a storage system.
