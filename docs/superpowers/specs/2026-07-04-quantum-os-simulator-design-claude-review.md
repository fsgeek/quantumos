Now the job. Four things, descending by how much I'd stop you, and the first one is the only one I'd genuinely hold implementation for.
1. The negative control's headline assertion has a hidden dependency that can make it fire wrongly in both directions. §15's perishability-attributable quantity is a difference-in-differences: [(S1−S0) under decay-on] − [(S1−S0) under decay-off], asserted "large by a stated factor." This is the right estimator and it's a real improvement over the naive gap-collapse I'd have accepted. But a DiD carries an assumption it doesn't state: that the two mechanisms compose additively enough that their interaction under decay-on isn't itself the thing you're measuring. Concretely — pre-generation and freshness-admission interact. Pre-generation changes the freshness distribution that admission control reads; admission control changes which pre-generated leases get used before they age. Under decay-on, that interaction is present; under decay-off, it's absent (admission's signal is constant, §15a). So the DiD isn't cleanly attributing to "perishability" — it's attributing to "perishability plus the perishability-mediated interaction between the two mechanisms," and those aren't separable by this estimator. The failure mode: a large DiD could be real perishability sensitivity, or it could be that the two mechanisms are strongly complementary only when decay is on, which is a different and weaker claim. You can't tell which from the headline number. The fix is cheap because the ladder already exists: report the DiD per rung, not just S1−S0. [(S0+admission − S0) under decay-on] − [same under decay-off] isolates admission's perishability-attributable increment with no interaction term, because pre-generation isn't in either arm. If that is large, the attribution is clean. If only the full-S1 DiD is large but the single-mechanism DiDs aren't, your effect lives in the interaction and the "perishability drives it" claim needs restating. This is the assertion-metamodel move one level deeper: the estimator has an invariant (additive separability) that isn't checked, and the ablation ladder is exactly the instrument to check it — but only if the DiD is computed at every rung, which §15 doesn't currently say. Right now §15 computes the increment at the admission rung (good) and the DiD at the S1 rung (exposed). Compute both at both.
2. The metamorphic checks are qualified to "fixed policy" — which quietly exempts the adaptive policy from metamorphic testing at all. §16.3 is careful and the carefulness is correct: adaptive admission games conditional metrics, so the checks are pinned to fixed policy, same seed, offered-normalized metrics. Good. But notice what "fixed policy" buys and costs. The metamorphic properties (faster decay must not increase goodput, tighter deadlines must not increase goodput) are asserted holding policy fixed and varying physics. That tests the engine's physical monotonicity. It does not test that S1 itself behaves monotonically — and S1 is a closed-loop adaptive controller, which is exactly the kind of thing that can respond non-monotonically to a monotone input (tighten a deadline slightly, admission sheds the right work, goodput rises; tighten more, it thrashes). That non-monotonicity might be a real finding about the policy or a bug in it, and the current metamorphic suite can't distinguish them because it never varies physics under S1. I'm not saying assert monotonicity under S1 — you're right that you can't, adaptive policy legitimately breaks it. I'm saying the spec should name that the metamorphic suite tests engine physics and not policy response, the same way §14 names what invariants don't cover and §16.2 names what limit checks don't cover. There's a scope statement missing here, and its absence is the one inconsistency in a document that is otherwise scrupulous about stating the limit of every check. The pattern of the spec is "every guard declares what it doesn't prove"; §16.3 is the guard that doesn't.
3. Closed-loop workload + common-random-numbers have a subtle tension the spec half-addresses. §10 is the best-engineered part of the document — key-addressed randomness so paired runs agree on shared semantic events regardless of draw order, with the hash() salting trap explicitly prohibited (that's a real bug someone would otherwise hit). But §9's workload is closed-loop: demand responds to system state. Under CRN, the keys are semantic identities — ("herald", round_id, endpoint_pair, attempt_no). In a closed-loop system, whether round 4417 even exists, and what endpoint pair it wants, depends on the policy's prior decisions. So S0 and S1 don't just make different draws — they generate different sets of semantic keys, because the workload they induce diverges. §10 acknowledges this ("draws that exist under only one policy simply have no counterpart") and calls it accepted divergence. It is — but it erodes the CRN guarantee more than the spec admits. The clean CRN promise ("same semantic event, same randomness") holds, but the fraction of events that are shared shrinks as policies diverge, and that fraction is unmeasured. Late in a run where S0 and S1 have driven the system to different states, they may share almost no semantic keys, and the paired comparison is silently back to being a two-sample statistical comparison for that portion. The fix isn't architectural — the design is right — it's observability: the trace should record, per paired comparison, the shared-key fraction over time, so you can see where CRN is actually buying variance reduction and where it's decayed to independent sampling. Otherwise you'll trust per-seed determinism in exactly the late-run regime where it's quietly gone. This is your own norm — don't let a silently-degraded guarantee masquerade as intact — applied to the variance-reduction machinery itself.
